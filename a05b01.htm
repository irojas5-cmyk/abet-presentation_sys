Feedback and Improvement #1:
Ongoing Feedback-Based Improvement of Courses Through University Course Evaluation Surveys, Department Course Update Surveys and Faculty Self Studies

The university conducts a standard student evaluation of teaching for each course at the university level. The average course evaluations from these student evaluations for each CHE faculty for the last five years are shown in Figure 4.1. All chemical engineering faculty have ratings higher than 3.25 which indicates greater than satisfactory to excellent ratings.

SSR Figure 4.1: Average Course Evaluations for ChE faculty for the last five years (2015-2020)

The Course Update Surveys (Assessment Method #5) are very detailed and give useful information to individual professors for improving their courses. This survey procedure is an automated online system programmed in-house with PHP.

We maintain on ongoing feedback loop for continuous improvement of existing courses. At the end of the semester, professors administer the Course Update Survey in their applicable courses toward the end of the semester. (This is in addition to the standard student evaluations of teaching conducted centrally at the university level.) After the semester is over, the professor receives by email the compiled survey results. The professor reflects on this student feedback and the feedback from the university student teaching evaluations. The Course Update Surveys and university teaching evaluations represent a regular and extensive consultation with our student constituency with tangibly positive effects on their courses and learning experiences. In addition, faculty respond to their course evaluations with recommendations and actions to improve their courses which are captured through the Faculty Performance Profiles done every July. Feedback and Improvement #2:
Introduction of CHE 451 (Renewable Energy Technologies) as a technical elective

The origin of CHE 451 and the program change that made it a technical elective lies in it first being offered as a CHE 492 Selected Topics in Chemical Engineering course in 2011 to bolster the curriculum in the alternative energy/renewable energy area. The course became popular with chemical engineering and other engineering major students and hence discussions and approval at a faculty meeting in spring 2016 led it to be delegated to the Undergraduate Committee to write a proposal to create it as a permanent 400-level technical elective course in the chemical engineering curriculum. The process for College level approval was started by filing forms for a new course proposal (CRS Outline) and a Program Change to the College of Engineering Educational Policy Committee (EPC). The committee reviewed the proposal during fall semester 2016 and approved the new CHE 451. The proposal then went to the UIC Sub-Committee on Education Policy (SCEP) and was approved in Spring 2017. Since becoming a permanent course, CHE 451 is offered once a year to both undergraduate and graduate students.

Feedback and Improvement #3:
Continuous Improvement based on SO Assessment and Evaluation

Student outcomes evaluated by the direct assessment method provide input for developing recommendations and actions for continuous improvement. From the periodic course rubric assessments, the instructors analyze the average scores and the number of students attaining a satisfactory, developing or unsatisfactory ratings. Based on these results, recommendations for continuous improvements are made and incorporated into the courses. Course rubric assessment, results, and continuous improvement actions are given below. The Senior Exit survey (assessment method #4) is also used as an indirect method to determine how well the students are perceiving that they are attaining the SOs. The ratings from the senior exit survey correlate well with the direct assessment methods.

The Engineering Expo judging rubrics are also used to assess the attainment of the SO’s by the students on a yearly basis. Based on the analysis from the rubrics on the capstone projects in Spring 2020, the CHE 397 senior design instructor, Professor Bilgin, recommended continuous improvement actions that will be incorporated into CHE 397 senior design course to improve the capstone project team's project.

Based on our Level 3 and Level 4 analysis of the attainment by different assessment methods and attainment for all SO’s, the department initiated a ‘Curriculum Day’ to discuss which aspects of the curriculum need of improvement and to recommend action items. The Curriculum Day was held in Fall 2018 with a majority of faculty present. The meeting consisted of faculty discussing the courses they teach and how to improve the content and student attainment of the SOs, with feedback from the rest of the faculty. In addition, there were 5 topic-specific lectures; ‘Best Practices in Teaching’ by Professor Wedgewood, ‘Best Practices in mentoring UGs for research’ by Professor Sharma, ‘Teaching Methods’ by Professor Bilgin, a lecture on one of our extracurricular student learning experiences, ‘Lessons from the Chem-E-Car Experience’, by Professor Singh and a Discussion on Improving student experience, led by Professors Berry and Nitsche. The meeting resulted in several action items to improve the curriculum. The Curriculum Day agenda and minutes with action items are given below. Feedback and Improvement #4:
Ongoing Feedback-Based Improvement of CHE 381/382 (Chemical Engineering Laboratory I and II)

Based on student feedback from the Course Evaluation Surveys, improvements to the Chemical Engineering Laboratory courses (CHE 381 and 382) has focused on three areas; new laboratory experiments and repair of existing lab equipment, revisions of lab manuals and procedures, and increasing the number of lab sections to allow for smaller lab teams. Three new lab experiments were introduced into the lab curriculum; Gas Absorption, Distillation and Process Control. A new distillation apparatus was purchased using a teaching-equipment procurement budget from the Dean’s Office in the College of Engineering in 2015. The new distillation column was a considerable upgrade from the previous distillation column, allowing the students to conduct more precise and meaningful experiments. A process control system (level control) was also purchased using the same teaching-equipment procurement budget to provide hands-on experience with process control principles and as a supplement to the Process Control course taught senior year. In addition, an existing but dormant gas absorption experiment was made operational to allow two unit operation principles to be taught in the same experiment; packed bed and gas absorption. Secondly, a process of re-writing the lab procedures and lab manuals has been undertaken each year, as well as improving lab safety and sustainability. For example, a lab procedure was revised to reduce mineral oil usage in the Liquid Extraction Experiment. Due to increasing enrollment, the one-section lab course was first split into two sections and then into four sections to allow for smaller lab teams of 4 members each. This has allowed for more personalized instruction and a better student learning experience.

Feedback and Improvement #5:
Ongoing Feedback Based Improvement of CHE 499 (Professional Development Seminar)

Based on informal student feedback, the CHE 499 Professional Development Seminar curriculum was revised in Fall 2018 to create a more comprehensive seminar series of 8-10 speakers throughout the semester. Invited speakers are invited from a variety of industries such as oil/gas, pharmaceutical, fine chemicals, food, patent law, alternative energy and semiconductors, and give talks on topics related to chemical engineering practice, professional development and job searching. The speakers are invited from the faculty's industry contacts and ChE alumni. The UIC Engineering Career Center also gives two seminars on job-related topics. Due to increasing enrollment, the oral exam and interview were changed in 2015 to a written examination that is given to the students as a take-home assignment. In addition, an ethics quiz was added in 2019 to the course based on discussions and feedback from the College Education Policy Committee to enhance student learning of ethics in the engineering curriculum.

    Ongoing Improvements to Extracurricular Student Learning Experiences: Career Development, Industry/Student Networking, Alumni/Student Connections

    In 2015, two undergraduate students approached the Engineering Career Center to ask how the College might improve the amount of companies attending the Engineering Career Fair hiring chemical engineers. This initial discussion led to the formation of an ad-hoc committee (Career Development committee) to brainstorm ideas on improving industry/student networking for undergraduate chemical engineering students. The ad-hoc committee consisted of the three undergraduate students, the director of the Engineering Career Center, and the Director of Undergraduate Studies in Chemical Engineering, and an ChE alumnus. After many discussions over a six-month period, the committee recommended expanding our industrial liaisons by creating an annual “Chemical Engineering Industry Day” event. The faculty voted to approve this event and it debuted in Fall 2015 as round-robin table discussions between the students and 18-20 professional chemical engineers working in a variety of industries. Industry Day has been a popular event and has continued each year in the Fall semester. After receiving feedback from the industry attendees and students in 2018, the event was improved to include a 6-speaker panel discussion and a high-top table networking hour at the end of the afternoon to promote further mingling and discussions between the students and the professional engineers. This activity fits into the department policy of providing the students with a learning experience to obtain the necessary skills and opportunities to become successful in their careers.

    Another initiative that was spearheaded by the Career Development committee and the student chapter of AICHE is the ChemE Forum. In its first year in 2016, the event was organized as ChemE week, with activities happening every day through one week in the spring. The next year, based on student feedback, the event was changed to the ChemE Forum and now consists of one afternoon of seminars on a broad area of topics that are pertinent to chemical engineering and professional development. The purpose of the event is to increase the awareness of UIC faculty research, and to provide students an opportunity to gain valuable insights into full-time employment. Seminar topics have included a safety tutorial by a safety consultant firm, a seminar on FE test preparation, and short presentations by the faculty on their research areas. The Forum is open to all undergraduates and we obtain faculty buy-in so that classes on that day are canceled as well as any quizzes or exams. Another unique aspect of the ChemE Forum is that an alumni mixer event occurs at the end where current students are encouraged to stay and talk to the UIC chemical engineer alumni. This event has also been very popular, and has continued each Spring semester since 2016. After student and alumni feedback, we combined the spring alumni mixer event to the ChemE Forum, and added an alumni panel discussion before the alumni mixer to discuss a myriad of pertinent topics, with questions from current students. The purpose is to provide more opportunities for alumni/student networking, and to increase the number of internships and jobs for students. During the last ChemE Forum in 2019, we added a Jeopardy game with chemical engineering and UIC related questions after the alumni panel discussion. The game has been a huge hit with the alumni and students. This activity fits into the department goal of increasing our sense of community and student identity as chemical engineers and to provide a more enriching educational experience to our students.

    Based on our collaborations with the Engineering Career Center through the Career Development committee, we have also enhanced industry/student and alumni/student interactions by strenthening our LinkedIn UIC Chemical Engineering group that was created in 2011, and becoming more involved in industry activities such as setting up a UIC Chemical Engineering booth at the ProFood Expo, and working closely with the Engineering Career Center to boost companies hiring ChE’s at the UIC Career Fair.

    Feedback and Improvement #8:
    Ongoing Improvements to the Safety Content of the Curriculum

    The incorporation of safety-related material into the Chemical Engineering curriculum was initiated in the last ABET cycle (2008-2014) to emphasize the importance of safety training for students and is now widely incorporated into many courses. The safety rubric is specifically tracked and assessed using SO #4-Safety (see Exhibit 4-6: Level 2 Analysis of Assessment Data). Specific SaChE tutorials from the AICHE website are now used regularly in several courses (CHE 201, 381, 382, 396 and 397) and include the following titles; “Introduction to Process Safety”, “Hazard Recognition”, “Dust Explosions”, and “Chemical Reactivity Hazards”. After suggestions from the senior design industry mentors, we also incorporated additional safety assignments into the CHE 396 and CHE 397 Senior Design I and II courses. These safety-related assignments discuss the hazards associated with the specific processes that the student groups are designing and include a HAZOP analysis in the form of a report and presentation. In addition, faculty self-study reflection from the CHE 381 and 382 Course Evaluation Surveys led to the addition of two safety assignments in 2015; an industrial R&D Chemical Hygiene Plan, and a Hazardous Chemical Safety Course reading assignment, with a quiz to grade comprehension.

    Feedback and Improvement #9:
    Improvement of the ENGR100-Freshmen Engineering Success Program (FESP) Chemical Engineering Breakout Session

    In 2013, the College of Engineering initiated a program in the freshmen engineering orientation course (ENGR 100) called the ‘Freshmen Engineering Success Program’ (FESP). The program is designed to increase retention of the freshmen engineering students, help them start identifying as engineers, and provide opportunities to meet faculty and upperclassmen in their majors. In addition, it is a requirement to be considered for the Guaranteed Paid Internship Program (GPIP) that is funded by the College. One part of FESP are 5-week breakout sessions within each specific major that include department advising, professionalism skills activities, and a team-based technical project. The FESP committee, consisting of faculty from each engineering major, periodically meet to discuss how to improve the breakout sessions and other aspects of FESP. Based on this feedback and discussions with the chemical engineering faculty, we have improved the technical project by making it a hands-on activity. Last year's project in 2019, the students built a solar cell, performed experiments, analyzed the data, and wrote a team report. In addition, the FESP committee is currently working to further improve the whole department breakout sessions and to include the transfer students in the success program.

    Feedback and Improvement #10:
    Future Improvements to the Curriculum: New Concentrations to the BS major and New Courses Aligned with the Concentrations.

    Based on discussions and feedback from our key constituencies and our faculty, a proposal has recently been approved by the chemical engineering faculty and the College, and is now being considered for approval by the university to modify our undergraduate program by adding five optional concentrations to the BS degree:

    • Concentration in Polymers and Molecular Engineering
    • Concentration in Energy and Environment
    • Concentration in Nanotechnology
    • Concentration in Process Automation
    • Concentration in Entrepreneurship

    The BS in chemical engineering major currently has one optional concentration, Biochemical Engineering. The addition of the five concentrations will strengthen the curriculum by providing more in-depth studies in key areas of chemical engineering; polymers, energy, environment and process automation. The department will also be more competitive with other chemical engineering programs by providing additional topic choices in emerging and multi-disciplinary fields of study; molecular engineering, nanotechnology and entrepreneurship.

    In addition to the proposed concentrations, several new courses have been proposed to provide the curriculum for the 5 new concentrations.

    • CHE 230 Molecular Systems in Chemical Engineering, and CHE 330 Polymer Science: These courses will introduce several topics that are important to traditional and emerging fields in chemical engineering; polymers, molecular engineering, macromolecular systems, and nanomaterials.
    • CHE 453 Fundamentals in Electrochemistry, CHE 454 Molecular and Macromolecular Engineering, CHE 455 Nanoscale Systems in Chemical Engineering, and CHE 457: Colloidal and Interfacial Phenomena: These courses will expand the list of technical electives in chemical engineering and will provide more comprehensive knowledge of the above topics; electrochemical engineering, polymers, molecular and macromolecular systems, and nanomaterials.

    The addition of these 5 courses will strengthen our core curriculum in key areas to modern chemical engineering practice where they play a central role in biological, chemical/industrial, environmental and pharmaceutical processes and technologies. Ultimately, they will provide the curriculum for the 5 new chemical engineering concentrations that will be proposed to our core program; Polymer and Molecular Engineering, Nanotechnology, Energy and Environment, Process Automation, and Intellectual Property
