Engineering and Innovation Building

The chemical engineering department moved into its new home in the Engineering Innovation Building (EIB) in summer 2019. The 57,500-square-foot-building is at the corner of Morgan and Taylor Streets on UIC’s east campus and is shared by the Chemical Engineering, Civil and Materials, and Mechanical Engineering Departments; the Chemical Engineering department is the largest tenant. The EIB is a state-of-the-art facility designed by Dewberry architects and built by Berglund Construction and is in the process of being LEED Gold certified. The new building features high-tech classrooms and laboratories, including vibration-dampened nanotechnology labs, biomedical research rooms, molecular engineering research labs, and a highly attuned darkroom for complex fluid imaging.

Faculty Offices:

    The Engineering Innovation Building contains 16 offices for chemical engineering faculty located on the first and second floors, two offices for teaching assistants (EIB 264 and 276), and one central office for the administrative staff (EIB 226). Faculty offices have a 43” LED 4KTV monitor mounted on the wall for student discussions. There is secure WIFI connectivity throughout the building.

Classrooms:

    There are two classrooms in EIB, Rooms 124 and 271. Both rooms are equipped with moveable tablet armchairs, high-definition video projectors and an Integrated Multimedia lectern with an installed computer, laptop connectivity, a document camera, a Blu-Ray player and internet connectivity. Room 124 has a state-of-the-art, lecture board containing a MultiTouch Video Wall System of eight, 55" LCD video wall displays. Room 271 is a state-of-the-art Computational Teaching Lab equipped with 21 Versatable Revolution Flip Top Computer Consoles for enhanced instruction and problem-based learning in numerical and computer courses. There is also a 75” LED 4KTV monitor for lectures as well as a whiteboard. Most chemical engineering courses are taught in these two classrooms, but other university classrooms with similar features are also utilized in order to provide the best class schedule for the students.

Laboratories:
  • Unit Operations Laboratory

    Unit Operations Laboratory Video

    The Unit Operations Laboratory for CHE 381 and 382 is located on the first floor of EIB in Room 128. A unique feature is that can be viewed from classroom EIB 124 by a large viewing window. The unit operations laboratory contains 12 functional chemical engineering process demonstrators supporting hands-on instruction of chemical engineering principles such as heat, mass and momentum transfer, fluid properties, and separations processes such as distillation, evaporation, absorption, filtration and extraction. The laboratory space is specifically designed as an instructional chemical engineering laboratory with dedicated workspaces for the process equipment and easy access to utilities such as 120V and 220V electricity, industrial hot and chilled water, purified water, compressed air, and vacuum lines. Eight moveable lab stations and two permanent lab bench areas are located throughout the lab and for the analytical equipment used in the process experiments. A new 48kW electric steam generator was installed in December 2019 and is plumbed directly to the appropriate process equipment. There are two fume hoods and four dropdown snorkel lines for chemical vapor control. The laboratory is furnished with two (2) computers connected to the distillation column and process control demonstrators, respectively. High-speed WiFi and ethernet connections to the UIC network are both available in the lab. Most students use personal laptop computers, tablets, or other devices. One section of the lab contains conference tables and chairs for the lab teams to work on their calculations and lab reports.

Computing Resources

The Chemical Engineering department has 18 computers running Windows 7 Professional for student/teaching use and one laser printer (Room 271) for student use. Additionally, our Faculty Research Laboratories include 39 computer workstations and PC clusters. These machines range from desktop PCs to Unix Workstations to a pair of 8 node LINUX clusters. These computers are utilized for both data analysis as well as simulations. All student/ teaching computers have installed Aspen, MATLAB, Microsoft Office 365 as well as other software specific to classes. The department of Chemical Engineering funds and maintains an exclusive 150 user license for the worldwide standard Aspen Chemical Simulation Software. Students can download the software onto their laptops as well as use the software in the computer lab (EIB 271) on the 18 Versatable Revolution Flip Top Computer Console workstations. In addition to the above, the students have access to an Epson Brightlink Pro interactive whiteboard in EIB 271.

In the Unit Operations Laboratory, there are 2 computers that are dedicated to running the distillation column and process control demonstrators. High-speed WIFI and ethernet connections to the UIC network are both available in the lab. Most students use personal laptop computers, tablets, or other devices.

Aside from computers dedicated to student use in the Chemical Engineering Building (CEB), computer labs are distributed throughout the campus – although the vast majority are concentrated on the east campus within easy walking distance of EIB. General information and a schedule for specific computer labs can be found in the following website:

http://accc.uic.edu/service/computer-labs

All these computers are installed with Excel and MATLAB and Microsoft Office 365, which are the main software packages that Chemical Engineering students would need.

For advanced computing needs and high-performance computing (HPC) on campus, the students have access to two clusters, Extreme and SABER. Extreme is a powerful machine with 3,500 cores, 24.5 Terabytes of memory and 1.25 Petabytes of local raw storage, of which 275 Terabytes is raw fast scratch storage. The cluster is composed of traditional, high memory and GPU computing nodes: 200 HPC worker nodes, 160 x Generation I: Intel Xeon E5-2670 @ 2.60 GHz, cache size of 20MB with 16 cores in each node, 128GB RAM and 1TB local storage; 40 x Generation II: Intel Xeon E5-2670 @2.50 GHz, cache size of 20MB with 20 cores per node, 128 GB RAM and 1TB local storage; 3 x Generation I High Memory Worker Nodes: Intel Xeon E5-4650L @ 2.60GHz, cache size of 20MB with 32 cores per node with 1TB RAM; 50 x Generation II GPU Worker Nodes; 3 x Login Nodes; and 2 x Admin Nodes. The traditional HPC and high memory worker nodes currently run CentOS 6.9. The GPU worker nodes currently run CentOS 7.5.

The Shared Analytics & Big-data Enterprise Resource (SABER) was introduced into the Advanced Cyberinfrastructure for Education and Research (ACER) HPC resource offerings in May 2017. In addition to traditional HPC workloads, this NSF-funded cluster provides access to various Big-Data/Analytics software packages. The SABER cluster consists of traditional high-performance computing nodes; 76 x HPC Nodes with Intel Xeon E5-2650 @ 2.20GHz; 24 x Cores/Node with 30 MB cache, 128GB RAM, 1TB Local Storage. Software on each node is CentOS 7.3.

    • Guidance

    Undergraduates performing research in faculty laboratories are required to attend a UIC Environmental Health and Safety Office (EHSO) laboratory safety training course before working in the laboratory. Information on the safety courses available can be found at:

    https://ehso.uic.edu/training/introduction-to-laboratory-safety-training/

    Undergraduates enrolled in the unit operations laboratory (CHE 381 and 382) are provided training and guidance by the instructor and teaching assistants on proper usage and safety precautions before they begin their experimental work on the chemical process demonstrators. This is initially done in the first lab period of the semester, and thereafter during ‘pre-lab’ laboratory periods. Additional guidance and oversight is provided while the lab teams are performing the experiments and afterwards to ensure proper equipment shut-off procedures.

    Makerspace staff provide guidance to students before they can use the machining tools and equipment. Three courses for more detailed instruction are also offered; ENGR 111 Additive Manufacturing, ENGR 112 Subtractive Machining and ENGR 214 Advanced Manufacturing Methods.
