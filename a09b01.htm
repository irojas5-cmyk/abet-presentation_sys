Institutional Support for the Chemical Engineering Program at the College and University Levels

Engineering Career Center

The Engineering Career Center (ECC) provides services and programs for both students enrolled in the College of Engineering and employers who recruit UIC engineering students, or hire engineers. The University provides a free job platform for both students and employers. Employers and ECC staff post openings, students upload resumes and apply for positions.

Students can schedule an appointment for an in-person one-on-one career advising meeting, they can also attend small group sessions for resume reviews, elevator pitch and interviewing skills. Students attend two specific engineering and computer science career fairs per academic year put on by the ECC; students also receive marketing information on other career fairs conducted by campus-wide career offices. ECC conducts presentations to classes including the engineering orientation course titled Engineering 100, and the Chemical Engineering Professional Development Seminar CHE 499.

The Chemical Engineering department and the ECC have a close working relationship. The ECC and Chemical Engineering department host two events during the academic year; the ChE Industry Day in the Fall semester and the ChemE Forum during the Spring semester. An alumni mixer event is a part of the ChemE forum and brings former students back to network with current chemical engineering students.

The ECC is also responsible for implementing the Guaranteed Paid Internship Program (GPIP) for new freshmen and new transfer students. This unique program offers an on-campus, paid research internship to those students who meet program requirements. For freshmen the internship occurs after their freshmen year and for transfer students it occurs before their last year on campus.

Minority Engineering Recruitment and Retention Program (MEERP)

The Minority Engineering Recruitment and Retention Program (MERRP), housed in the College of Engineering, has, as its major effort, to increase in the number of African American, Latino, and Native American students earning engineering degrees. The Program staff is composed of professionals with backgrounds in higher education, engineering, and engineering education. MERRP is the foundation of the minority engineering student experience at UIC. Most minority engineering students are introduced to MERRP through the college orientation program and the engineering student orientation course, ENGR 100. MERRP has supported the student chapters of the Society of Hispanic Professional Engineers (SHPE) and the National Society of Black Engineers (NSBE) and has been a source of experience and advice for both groups. The hallmark of MERRP is Supplemental Instruction (SI) and the “academic family” atmosphere.

Women in Science and Engineering Program (WISE)

The goal of the UIC Women in Science and Engineering Program, WISE, is to increase the number of women students pursuing and graduating in science, technology, engineering and math, STEM, disciplines, and to promote the recruitment, retention and advancement of women who have chosen academic careers. Nationally, retention rates for women STEM students fall far behind those of their male counterparts, and women consistently make up less than 20% of tenured STEM faculty. WISE supports women undergrad/graduate students and faculty in STEM by sponsoring activities that foster a positive educational and professional environment, and enable excellence in scholarship, teaching and service. Since 2016, the chemical engineering department has maintained 35 to 40% female enrollment in our undergraduate program. Many of the Chemical Engineering women students participate in WISE activities and are active members in the Society of Women Engineering (SWE).

Program Budget and Financial Support

The institutional budget for the Department of Chemical Engineering consists of two parts, personnel and the operating costs. The personnel costs are those for salaries of faculty, union staff members, academic professionals and teaching assistants. The budgeted amount for these salaries increases each year by an average percentage mandated by the University. These increases have so far been to retain qualified personnel in the department. The budget of the College is currently in part tied to the enrolment in Engineering and the tuition revenue generated by the enrolled students. While the Department budget is not tied directly to enrolments, it is heavily influenced by it. The department has had stable student enrolments in the last few years. A few years ago, we increased the number of TAs in the department from 9 to 16 to reflect an increase in our enrollments. The budget for the operating costs has not increased for the last few years and due to poor statewide economic conditions and has in fact decreased slightly in the last fiscal year. The department has three accounts where it generates additional revenue: (a) The Indirect Cost account (the fraction of the overhead funds available from research grants that is returned to the department), (b) Gift account (gift funds given by benefactors of the department) and (c) Educational Program account (tuition revenue from other educations programs, such as China 3+2, India-MS programs). These additional funds have therefore also been used to ensure that the program objectives are met. Some examples of how these funds have been used in meeting program objectives are given below:

  1. Updating Departmental Computing Services: The funds have been used to purchase site licenses for software needed by our students and faculty for their classroom activities, such as ASPEN Plus, and CHEMCAD.

  2. New Unit Operations Laboratory Equipment: Distillation column, process control equipment, digital balance, digital hygrometer, lab computers.

  3. Professional Development Supplement: The faculty receive a professional development funds from the university (~$900 for non-tenure-track and $1200 for tenure-track). The above mentioned funds have been used to supplement the funds from university for teaching workshops, and conference visits.

  4. Startup Packages: The department hired three assistant professors and one associate professor in 2016. The departmental share of the startup funds is covered by these funds.

The current sources of financial support for the Department are the State of Illinois with additional revenue mentioned above. The state funding covers faculty and staff salary, as well as some additional non-permanent positions such as Teaching Assistants and part-time student workers. This portion of the budget is fixed each year by the University -- changes made are generally consistent with the support provided to the University by the State of Illinois. In addition the Department receives a portion of the Indirect Cost Recovery (ICR) from research grants housed in the department, or those in which the department faculty participate. Currently the University returns 47.5% of the overhead cost to the College of Engineering, and this is then divided equally between the College and the Department. The Department actively works with the Development Office of the College and University to raise gifts from alumni and local and national businesses with an interest in our Department and University. These gifts when they are discretionary are also used to fund special projects in the department including sometimes emergency repairs to equipment in the UG Laboratories that has not been budgeted. Our development activities have resulted in one major gift and several smaller gifts. The major gift is being used for an endowed professorship position in the department

While there has been no increase in state budget over a period of time, the alternate revenue streams have seen an increase driven by a steady increase in our student enrollments and our international programs. This together with careful budgetary management in general by the Dean’s office has allowed us to providing a high quality education for the undergraduate students.

Further, in 2018, we started the process on constructing a $45M building (Engineering Innovation Building) to house the entire department. The department moved into EIB in August 2019. The building has a state-of-the-art classroom, unlike any other with a large touch-screen. Further, the room is annexed to our unit-operations laboratory via a large window through which students in the lecture room can see the various equipment; a model that was inspired by the medical observatory classrooms. The building also has student learning spaces spread throughout the building, including a computational room, library, lounge, and atrium seating space.

College alumni donor funding for the scholarships and computer software is another example of support for our program. Strong support enables our effectiveness in teaching and learning. The College conducts surveys of teaching and advising every semester, and the Chemical Engineering department usually ranks the highest in these surveys. Another measure of the quality of instruction we have provided to our students is the Annual Engineering Expo. In the past years, our students have won a disproportionately higher share of awards. This year, due to COVID-19 stay-at-home guidelines, we had a virtual Expo, and although no awards were given, the chemical engineering teams received high judge scores for their projects.
